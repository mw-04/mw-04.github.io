DANL 310 Project: Star Classification

Introduction

The Universe contains billions and trillions of stars; they are among the most recognizable objects in space. Stars tend to be massive and hot, leading to the formation of heavier elements like oxygen via nuclear fusion. Another thing that stars do is emit light; as light moves through expanding space, it reddens, and with this reddening, called redshift, we can determine cosmological distances and even the age of the Universe. By analyzing sky surveys of stars and using their spectra, we can determine characteristics such as size, temperature, and luminosity and classify them into evolutionary stages via color and type. This project uses stellar classification data to explore the life cycles of stars and to visualize the fundamental physical relationships, such as the Stefan-Boltzmann Law, that govern the behavior of matter at the cosmic and atomic scales.

Data

This data comes from Kaggle; here is the link: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/deepu1109/star-dataset.

This is a collection of data points from varying surveys/sources, containing the following properties: temperature, size, luminosity, magnitude, type, color, and class.

Temperature is the kinetic energy of particles within these stars; temperature is measured in Kelvin (K).

\[\lambda_{max} = \frac{b}{T}\]

This is Wien’s Displacement Law; this determines the peak wavelength of the star in a spectrum.

By assuming that stars are pretty spherical, their size is their radius. In this dataset, it is the ratio between the actual size and the solar radius. The Sun has a radius of \(6.597 \times 10^{8} \text {m}\).

Luminosity is the power of the light emitted from the star. In this data, it is the ratio between its actual and solar luminosities; the luminosity of the Sun is \(3.828 \times 10^{26} \text {W}\).

\[L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T^4\]

This is Stefann-Boltzmann Law, which relates luminosity to the size and tempeature. In solar quantities this is the following formula:

\[\frac{L}{L_{\odot}} = \left(\frac{R}{R_{\odot}}\right)^2 \left(\frac{T}{T_{\odot}}\right)^4\]

In astronomy, magnitude has two names: apparent and absolute. Apparent magnitude is the apparent brightness to Earth of an object 10 parsecs (\(3.086 \times 10^{17} \text {m}\)) away. Absolute magnitude is the intrinsic brightness from the luminosity.

\[m - M_v = 5 \log_{10}(d) - 5\]

Now this is distance modulus, which relates absolute and apparent magnitudes. d is distance in parsecs.

Stellar type is based on the Morgan-Keenan System (MK). The letters are O, B, A, F, G, K, M, where O stars are the hottest while M stars are the coolest.

Stellar color is self-explanatory– it’s the color of the star, often related to its surface temperature. Stars are typically blue, white, yellow, orange, and red, with some “intermediates” such as blue-white and yellow-white. Bluer stars are hotter while redder stars are cooler.

Finally, there are classes based on color and size. The six classes are: brown, red, and white dwarves, main sequence, supergiants, and hypergiants. The three dwarves are based on color (and temperature), while super and hypergiants can be any color; they are just the most luminous and massive. Main sequence stars are at a stage of evolution, spending most of their time fusing hydrogen into helium and eventually heavier elements.

Color Justification:

For data determined scaling relations, I used plasma as it is color blind friendly, maintaining contrast along wide numerical ranges. It is shown that the luminosity-radius scaling and HR diagrams were stars are measured and observed on an exponential (10^{x}) scale.

Data that focused on characteristics of population used a variation of the “PuRd” (purple to red) color palette, as these are sequential and signify intensity; for the heat map, a color gradient from white to hot pink was used. One example is the temperature boxplot distribution, which automatically sorted the least luminous brown dwarves to a pale color, while the most luminous hypergiants were a deep brown color. For the population histogram, colors were mapped out manually to align with the physical color (white dwarves were the palest color, while the giants were the purples, as super and hypergiants tend to be blue and red.

Interactive and Animated Plots:

I decided to make the temperature distribution interactive because for some classes, they have a small range, so it can be hard to see and understand the distribution. By simply hovering over each boxplot, one sees the IQR, min, and max easily. I also decided to make a density plot, which serves as a combination of the population histogram and HR diagram. The population histogram shows the amount of each category, while the HR diagram is like the life mapping of stars.

Although HR shows luminosity, luminosity and absolute magnitude are related by the following: \[M_v = M_{\odot} - 2.5 \log_{10}\left(\frac{L}{L_{\odot}}\right)\]

Where \(M_{\odot}\) is the absolute magnitude of the Sun (4.83). Magnitude is reversed; a more negative number means a more luminous star(super/hypergiants), while a more positive number is a less luminous star (brown/red dwarfs).

Conclusion

The purpose of this activity demonstrated how data analysis is important in astronomy, where there are large data samples and a need to classify elements in said samples. For this example of stars, a common type of activity astrophysicists do, it helps bridge physical theory to the phenomena observed.

Some main takeaways were that there are 6 classes, with red stars being the most frequent color, appearing 114 times, which means M types (red dwarfs) are the most common in this set. For some main sequence stars that are particularly large and luminous (O and B stars), for example, in the temperature distribution, the average temperature was ~16,000 K, while our Sun (also a main sequence star) is ~5,000 K. However, this is simply one sample, and the mapping of star types and spectral classes is made from many samples and surveys, which eventually averages out characteristics. # Project Checklist

### Color Justification:

For data determined scaling relations, I used plasma as it is color blind friendly, maintaining contrast along wide numerical ranges. It is shown that the luminosity-radius scaling and HR diagrams were stars are measured and observed on an exponential (10\^{x}) scale.

Data that focused on characteristics of population used a variation of the "PuRd" (purple to red) color palette, as these are sequential and signify intensity; for the heat map, a color gradient from white to hot pink was used. One example is the temperature boxplot distribution, which automatically sorted the least luminous brown dwarves to a pale color, while the most luminous hypergiants were a deep brown color. For the population histogram, colors were mapped out manually to align with the physical color (white dwarves were the palest color, while the giants were the purples, as super and hypergiants tend to be blue and red.

### Interactive and Animated Plots:

I decided to make the temperature distribution interactive because for some classes, they have a small range, so it can be hard to see and understand the distribution. By simply hovering over each boxplot, one sees the IQR, min, and max easily. I also decided to make a density plot, which serves as a combination of the population histogram and HR diagram. The population histogram shows the amount of each category, while the HR diagram is like the life mapping of stars.

Although HR shows luminosity, luminosity and absolute magnitude are related by the following: $$M_v = M_{\odot} - 2.5 \log_{10}\left(\frac{L}{L_{\odot}}\right)$$

Where $M_{\odot}$ is the absolute magnitude of the Sun (4.83). Magnitude is reversed; a more negative number means a more luminous star(super/hypergiants), while a more positive number is a less luminous star (brown/red dwarfs).
